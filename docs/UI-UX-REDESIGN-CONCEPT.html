Article: Young volunteers in the city

Reading Part 3 · Choose the correct answer for each question

READING TEXT

Young volunteers in the city

Every Saturday morning, a group of students from Hill Park School meet outside the city library. They are not there to borrow books. Instead, they collect gloves, bags and maps before walking to different parts of the city to help local community projects.

Fifteen-year-old Maya joined the group last autumn. At first, she only wanted to spend more time with her friends. After a few weeks, however, she realised that the work was more interesting than she expected. She helped plant flowers near a bus station, painted a wall at a youth centre and showed younger children how to sort paper and plastic for recycling.

The project leader, Mr Lane, says the most important part is not the number of hours students work. "They learn to notice what is happening around them," he explains. "Many teenagers travel through the city every day, but they do not always feel responsible for it. Volunteering changes that."

Parents have also noticed a difference. Some students now remind their families to save water or repair old things instead of buying new ones. Others have become more confident when speaking to adults, because they often have to ask shop owners or local residents for information during a task.

The group is planning a new project for the summer. They want to create a walking guide for visitors that includes quiet green spaces, free sports areas and small museums that many tourists miss. Maya says she is looking forward to it because it will combine photography, writing and teamwork.
